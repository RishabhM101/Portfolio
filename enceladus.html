A Combined Satellite And Lander Mission To Investigate The Geology And Astrobiology Of Enceladus

Few worlds in our solar system are as compelling as Saturn’s icy ocean moon Enceladus. It is known for its highly reflective surface, which is primarily due to its fresh, clean ice cover. Underneath its icy shell, Enceladus harbors a liquid water ocean.

What sets Enceladus apart is its active cryovolcanism. It ejects plumes of its oceanic water into space, providing a unique opportunity for spacecraft to sample it directly.

Enceladus has it all !

These samples have revealed that Enceladus possesses most of the chemical ingredients necessary for life. Even phosphorus — the rarest of six elements upon which life as we know it depends.

This raises the possibility that life could exist on Enceladus.

This paper presents a conceptual mission design study focused on exploring both the atmosphere and surface of Enceladus. The primary objective of the orbiter is to establish a stable orbit around the moon, enabling a comprehensive assessment of its atmospheric and surface conditions. Equipped with a sample collection unit, the orbiter is designed to pass through the geysers, capturing gases and particulates emitted by the moon. Subsequently, these samples will undergo thorough analysis to detect potential signs of life.

Additionally, the satellite will have gradiometer to study the differentiation in Enceladus’s core. Complementing this, the lander carries a rover equipped with a seismometer, tasked with mapping the moon's internal structure. Furthermore, the rover conducts spectral analyses to understand the chemical composition of the surface.

My Current Work On The Mission:

1] To identify a launch vehicle and craft a trajectory that takes full advantage of aerogravity assist from the inner planets and Saturn’s moons, we can carefully maneuver the spacecraft through the atmosphere of Saturn’s moons to achieve controlled deceleration and capture into orbit around Enceladus, a technique known as aerocapture. This not only conserves propellant but also facilitates efficient orbital insertion.

2] Given that the primary objective of this mission is to detect signs of life, my current focus involves conducting a detailed assessment of OWLS to determine its suitability for our mission's specific requirements.

The Ocean Worlds Life Surveyor (OWLS) is an integrated, portable, and autonomous life-detection instrument suite designed to identify and characterize life on ocean worlds. Once a water sample is collected, OWLS identifies evidence for life at molecular and cellular levels by combining chemical analysis with high-resolution microscopy.

OWLS will integrate two subsystems: the Organic Capillary Electrophoresis Analysis System (OCEANS) and the Extant Life Volumetric Imaging System (ELVIS).
